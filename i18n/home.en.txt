A mountain’s grandeur lies not in its height, but in the presence of a sage. A river’s spirit lies not in its depth, but in the presence of a dragon. This may be a humble dwelling, yet my virtue makes it serene.

Moss traces the steps in lush green, while grass casts shadows upon the curtain’s screen. Here, scholars engage in refined discourse—no place for the unlettered and coarse. One may pluck the zither’s purest strings or delve into sacred golden scripts. No discordant tunes disturb the ears, nor do tedious documents burden the form.

This is like Zhuge’s thatched retreat in Nanyang, or Ziyun’s pavilion in Western Shu. As Confucius once asked: "How is here crude?"
